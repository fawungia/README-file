The Truth About Eye Creams

By Editor • June 17 5 Min Read

The beauty industry loves to convince us that if we don't have a highly specific product for every square inch of our faces, we are doing something wrong. Eye cream is the clearest example of this. It's one of the most reliably purchased items in any routine, and also one of the most misunderstood. So let's ask the question honestly: do you actually need a separate eye cream, or is it just a smaller jar of your moisturizer with a bigger price tag?

The answer is more nuanced than either camp — the "eye cream is a scam" crowd or the "you need twelve steps" crowd — wants it to be. The skin under your eyes isn't a different organ. It's the same skin, just thinner, with less oil production and more visible movement. That distinction matters more than the marketing does.

Why the Under-Eye Area Feels "Different"

The skin around your eyes has fewer sebaceous glands and a thinner dermis than the rest of your face, which is why it shows dehydration, sun damage, and fatigue faster than anywhere else. It's also constantly moving — blinking roughly 15,000 times a day — which is why fine lines tend to show up there first.

None of that means it needs a fundamentally different ingredient list. It means it needs the same good ingredients, applied with more restraint. A rich, actives-heavy face cream can genuinely irritate this area, not because the actives are wrong, but because the barrier here is thinner and less forgiving of excess.

When a Dedicated Eye Cream Actually Earns Its Place

This is where I'll push back on the "it's all a scam" take, because it isn't always true. There are two specific concerns where a formulated eye product is worth the separate purchase:

  • Dark circles and puffiness: caffeine and niacinamide, at concentrations formulated for this thinner skin, can meaningfully reduce morning puffiness by constricting blood vessels and supporting the microcapillaries under the eye.
  • Deep, established crow's feet: a retinoid formulated at eye-safe strength can genuinely improve texture here — but it needs to be gentler than what you'd use on the rest of your face, or you risk irritating one of the most reactive areas on your body.

If neither of those describes your actual concern, you are very likely paying for packaging, not efficacy.

The Marketing Trick Nobody Talks About

Here's the honest, slightly cynical truth: a large number of "eye creams" on the market are simply the brand's standard moisturizer, repackaged into a smaller jar with a higher per-ounce price and a claim about "delicate skin." Check the ingredient list against the brand's regular face moisturizer next time you're in a store. More often than you'd expect, they're nearly identical.

That doesn't mean every eye cream is a scam — the caffeine and gentle-retinoid formulas above are real, targeted chemistry. It means the burden of proof is on the product, not on your skepticism. Read the ingredient list before you read the claims on the box.

The bottom line: if your current routine is already working and you have no specific under-eye concern, save your money. If you're dealing with genuine puffiness or established fine lines, buy something formulated for that concern specifically — not just something in a smaller jar.
